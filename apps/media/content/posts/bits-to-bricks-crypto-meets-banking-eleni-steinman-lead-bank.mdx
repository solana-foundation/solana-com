[Eleni Steinman](http://linkedin.com/in/elenisteinman) spent years building Loop Crypto to get merchants to accept stablecoins directly. It didn't work the way she expected. Now she runs stablecoin strategy inside a 97-year-old chartered bank in Kansas City, and the use cases that actually landed look nothing like what she originally imagined.

[Lead Bank](https://www.lead.bank) was acquired in 2022 by former Square Capital operators who understood firsthand how painful it is for fintechs to find a banking partner willing to move at their speed. Rather than layer on top of someone else's charter, they bought one. Steinman joined as Head of Stablecoins, [bringing her Loop Crypto team with her](https://www.theblock.co/post/382124/a16z-backed-lead-bank-adds-loop-crypto-to-inner-circle-with-eye-on-scaling-stablecoins-and-payments).

She sat down with [Amira Valliani on Bits to Bricks](https://solana.com/podcasts/bits-to-bricks-with-amira-valliani/episodes/crypto-meets-banking-a-new-era-with-eleni-steinman-lead-bank-e3h5vqc) to talk about what stablecoins can actually do inside a regulated bank, and where the technology still falls short.

## **A bank built like a protocol**

Lead Bank  is a federally chartered, Missouri-regulated bank, meaning it holds a bank charter: a license from regulators that allows it to take deposits, make loans, and operate as a full banking institution. Most fintechs skip that step and partner with a chartered bank instead. Lead's founders went the other way. They acquired the bank because they wanted to own the compliance and infrastructure layer directly. Their client list now includes Brex, Ramp, Revolut, and Affirm.

Owning the banking charter, Steinman says, is like running your own validator instead of relying on an RPC provider. You get visibility into the full lifecycle.

"We don't wait till the end of the month to go back and look at all of our transactions and see if there was a compliance issue," Steinman said. "We actually embed it within the product itself so that we can catch issues early on. That is only possible when you own the full stack."

Lead's tech is built in the smallest possible components: accounts, funding mechanisms, payment rails. Instead of handing clients a rigid product, they assemble the pieces differently for each one. Steinman likens it to DeFi's composable building blocks, where wallets, swaps, and yield mechanisms  all snap together.

That modularity is how Lead serves fintechs that traditional banks aren't set up to serve. Not because the risk is higher, but because the use cases are unfamiliar. "Sometimes the things that we do seem riskier than they are because they're poorly understood," Steinman said. Real-time transaction monitoring, built into the product rather than bolted on after the fact, lets Lead get comfortable with clients other banks would pass on.

## **The merchant acceptance dead end**

Before Lead Bank, Steinman co-founded Loop Crypto in 2022 on a straightforward premise: as more value grew on-chain, people would want to spend it directly.

[Loop Crypto](https://www.lead.bank/blog-posts/loop-crypto-joins-lead) built a payment processor that enables businesses and e-commerce platforms to use stablecoins for  faster, more efficient, and secure payments. Their model was to go to merchants, make it easy to accept stablecoins, and cut out the card networks.

It didn't play out that way. "I thought there'd be acceptance of stablecoins much faster than we'll see," Steinman said. Merchant adoption was hard. "One day I do think we'll get there. But I can't tell you how far into the future that will be."

The last mile is being solved differently than she expected. Orchestrators like[ Bridge](https://www.bridge.xyz),[ BVNK](https://bvnk.com), and [Reap](https://reap.global/) move value over stablecoin rails, then convert to local currency at the destination. The merchant never knows a stablecoin was involved. Card issuers do something similar: a customer holds a stablecoin balance, swipes a card, and the merchant gets paid in dollars.

"The great unlock of stablecoins is really more about moving value fast," Steinman said, "which is a nuanced difference between making a payment and getting something of value to its end destination."

## **Where stablecoins actually save money**

Capital efficiency is the first place where stablecoins save money. In traditional banking, moving money between a checking account and a money market fund takes two to three days. During that window, the funds earn nothing. With stablecoins, that capital moves instantly between yield-bearing positions and spendable cash.

The second is in card network settlement. Banks that issue cards must pay the card networks every day for the previous day's transactions. Normally that means sending a wire, but wires don't process on weekends. So the networks require banks to post collateral covering Saturday through Monday, just to guarantee they'll get paid.

In December 2025,[ Visa launched USDC settlement in the US](https://usa.visa.com/about-visa/newsroom/press-releases.releaseId.21951.html), after a successful pilot program with[ $3.5 billion in annualized volume](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2025/12/16/visa-brings-circle-s-usdc-settlement-to-u-s-banks-following-usd3-5-billion-stablecoin-pilot). Lead Bank was one of the first participating banks.

Lead Bank now settles with the networks using stablecoins on Solana, including weekends and holidays. Bridge sends USDC on Solana to Lead, and Lead forwards the funds to the network. No off-ramping required. Clients don't need to be crypto companies to benefit either:  Lead can mint stablecoins from dollar deposits and settle on their behalf.

"With stablecoins now, we can pay the network on Saturday or Sunday. And if Monday's a holiday, we can still pay them on Monday," Steinman said. "That's a huge cost savings for our customers."

## **Fiat and stablecoin rails, side by side**

Steinman isn't predicting a crypto takeover of banking. Her view is more measured.

"I could see a world where there are a lot more stablecoin-based balances, but the end customer doesn't know," she said. An account might show $1,000, but whether that's sitting in fiat or stablecoins under the hood would be invisible to the user. When they send money to a relative overseas, the system picks whichever rail is faster and cheaper.

Her advice to regional banks exploring stablecoins is to start with the customer problem, not the technology. "Know why you're doing it. Be very clear about what is the problem we're trying to solve by using a stablecoin and who are we trying to solve this problem for."

"To convince a 100-year-old institution to completely change their infrastructure overnight is probably a hard sell. To convince someone to change it over the next 50 years, that seems more possible.  It's just a question of the time horizon for changes to take place." Lead Bank, a nearly century-old charter, is already settling with Visa on Solana. That says more about where this is headed than any prediction.
